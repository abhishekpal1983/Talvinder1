0. How to Use This Manual

This manual is the single source of truth for selling Pragmatic AI: The AI Product Sprint. It is organized in three parts: Product Knowledge (what the program actually is and delivers), How to Sell (scripts, discovery, objection handling), and Guardrails & Ops (what you can and cannot say, plus the operational checklists you'll use daily).

Read Sections 1–14 fully before taking your first call — this is the product knowledge that separates a confident rep from one who is guessing. Sections 15–20 are your working scripts. Section 21 is non-negotiable: it governs every claim you're allowed to make. Sections 23–26 are quick-reference tools for live calls. Section 27 is a self-check to confirm you're ready.

Golden rule: Sell the guided build system, not just the course content. The student is buying a structured path to build, test, host, demo, and explain a useful AI tool for their own work — not a video library, not a job guarantee, not unlimited cloud credits.

1. The Offer in One Sentence

Pragmatic AI: The AI Product Sprint is a 12-week live cohort where mid-career professionals build, test, and ship a useful AI tool for their own work, with guided support, approved hosting, starter model/API credits, and a final demo they can show credibly.

The student does not leave with only prompts, theory, or a certificate. They leave with a working AI product or internal tool, a demo, a portfolio artifact, and a scorecard that shows where the tool works, where it fails, and how it compares with a baseline such as raw ChatGPT or Claude.

The program is built for people who already understand a real workflow — in product, marketing, sales, operations, consulting, finance, HR, education, or as founders — and helps them turn that workflow knowledge into a usable AI tool.

4. Who It's For / Not For

Strongest fit

  • Mid-career professionals, roughly 8–20 years of experience, who understand a recurring business task deeply and want to become more credible with AI at work.
  • Founders, consultants, and operators who want a lightweight AI product or internal tool without hiring an engineering team first.
  • People who tried ChatGPT/Claude but stopped because outputs felt generic, ungrounded, hard to trust, or disconnected from their real workflow.

Not a fit

  • Someone who wants a passive video course — this is a live build program with weekly capstone work.
  • Someone who wants a job-placement guarantee — the outcome is capability, a shipped artifact, and possible showcase visibility, not placement.
  • Senior ML engineers wanting model internals, research papers, or deep infrastructure — they can join via the Advanced Lane, but the center of gravity is the AI-capable operator, not the ML researcher.

5. The Four Outcomes Every Student Leaves With

  1. A working AI product or internal tool — solves a real task, uses real data/workflow knowledge, usable by the student or a real workplace user.
  2. A baseline scorecard — compares the student's tool against raw ChatGPT/Claude/another agreed baseline on the same task. We are not promising every student beats a generic chatbot — we're promising every student can explain where their tool works better, where it fails, and what needs improvement.
  3. A demo and portfolio artifact — shows the tool, the problem, the before/after workflow, and how the student tested whether the AI output can be trusted.
  4. A professional showcase story — "I did not just learn AI. I built a useful AI tool for my domain, tested it, shipped it, and can explain when to trust it." If selected, they can present to CEOs/CTOs/operators/founders/hiring managers without overclaiming.

6. Program Format & Delivery Model

This is a live cohort, not a content library. Default format: 12 weeks plus Week 0 onboarding/prework.

The two-session weekly rhythm

SessionPurpose
Live Session 1 — Concept & DemonstrationTeaches the week's topic: concept, pattern, tool choices, good vs. bad implementation examples, and a live demo. Uses product-agnostic language for any internal shipped system referenced.
Live Session 2 — Guided Build ClinicStudents apply the concept to their own capstone: build, decide, share blockers, get guidance from Talvinder/mentors/team. Not a repeat lecture — it converts the topic into visible capstone progress.

The weekly checkpoint is not a live session. It's the submitted artifact at end of week — proof the student applied the topic. Checkpoints determine Ship Guarantee / Capstone Rescue eligibility.

An optional check-in / to-do session can be added for blocked/behind students — it is a structured working room (blocker triage, checkpoint clarification, next actions), never a place where new curriculum is taught.

Between live sessions, students spend 3–5 hours on capstone work. There are weekly office hours for unresolved blockers, and an async community structured by week and capstone lane.

7. Build Lanes

One curriculum, three implementation depths. Everyone learns the same product thinking, agent patterns, grounding, evaluation, model choice, governance, deployment, and demo discipline — only the implementation depth changes.

Operator Lane — for non-coders & domain experts

Typical tools: ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, custom GPT-style tools, Claude Projects, Gemini Gems, spreadsheets, Airtable, Notion, Google Drive, Zapier, Make, n8n, Retool, or another approved no-code interface.

Cost note: guided toward free tiers and approved tools/hosting. Paid subscriptions outside the approved stack are the student's cost.

Builder Lane — for PMs, analysts, consultants, semi-technical professionals

Typical tools: Cursor, Claude Code, lightweight Python/JS templates, Streamlit, Vercel, Supabase, Retool, API wrappers, hosted vector search, spreadsheet-based evaluation templates.

Cost note: program covers approved deployment hosting + starter API/model credits. Does not cover personal coding-tool subscriptions (Cursor, Claude Code, etc.) unless explicitly added before launch.

Founder / Advanced Lane — for founders, consultants, technical students wanting a custom/client-ready MVP

Typical build: simple frontend, API layer, retrieval over a structured corpus, model routing, logging, basic auth, evaluation harnesses, deployment to a lightweight production-like environment.

Cost note: covers approved capstone hosting + starter credits. Custom domains, special infrastructure, heavy usage, paid observability, external databases, or production-grade deployments are separate unless approved case by case.

Lane choice happens in Week 0 via the Capstone Brief and can be adjusted by Week 2 if the first scope is too easy, too technical, too costly, or too vague.

8. Week-by-Week Curriculum

Use this section to speak credibly about "what actually happens each week" — prospects will ask.

Week 0 — Onboarding, Tool Setup, Cost Policy & Capstone Selection

Goal: enter Week 1 with an approved capstone direction, the right build lane, minimum tools set up, and clarity on covered vs. non-covered costs.

Checkpoint: Capstone Brief v0 (user, painful task, workflow, data, success metric, baseline result, risk level, build lane, expected API usage, voice needs, paid-tool dependency).

Week 1 — Find the Product Worth Building

Goal: turn a vague AI idea into a specific, cost-aware product problem shippable in 12 weeks.

Checkpoint: Capstone Brief v1 (one user, one workflow, one task, one success metric, five sample inputs, baseline output, build lane, first cost/credit note).

Week 2 — Build the First Working AI Agent or Workflow

Goal: a first working version performing one useful task.

Checkpoint: first working demo (link/recording/walkthrough) + flag of any paid subscription or program credit used.

Week 3 — Make the Workflow Multi-Step and Reliable

Goal: handle more than one step and recover from simple failures.

Checkpoint: workflow map + working multi-step demo with at least one check, retry, fallback, or human-review step.

Week 4 — Ground the Tool in Real Data

Goal: connect the tool to a real source of truth instead of relying on model memory.

Checkpoint: grounded version of the tool, source of truth, 5–10 test cases, ≥2 examples of evidence-based answers.

Week 5 — Stop the Tool from Making Unsupported Claims

Goal: add a trust layer catching unsupported/incomplete/overconfident answers.

Checkpoint: grounding & trust test — supported answers, caught unsupported answers, one hallucination-risk improvement.

Week 6 — Build the Evaluation Scorecard Signature Week

Goal: prove whether the tool is actually good for the chosen task.

Checkpoint: Scorecard v1 — task, rubric, test cases, baseline output, tool output, scores, failure notes, next-improvement decision, approximate API usage.

The strongest student statement after this week: "I know how my tool performs, where it beats a generic chatbot, where it does not, and what to fix next."

Week 7 — Choose the Right Model at the Right Cost

Goal: make an informed model choice instead of defaulting to the priciest/most popular model.

Checkpoint: model choice note — options tested, quality/cost difference, final decision, whether extra credit approval is needed.

Week 8 — Make the Tool Safe Enough to Use

Goal: add practical governance so the tool doesn't create avoidable risk.

Checkpoint: safety checklist + guardrail demo — top risks, controls added, one working guardrail test, one cost/usage protection note.

Week 9 — Ship the Tool for Real Use

Goal: turn the capstone into something another person can open and use via the approved hosting path.

Checkpoint: live link / usable workflow / demo environment + access instructions, walkthrough, hosting path used, unresolved risks.

Week 10 — Test with a Real User and Capture Early Proof

Goal: learn what breaks with a real user and start capturing credible outcome proof.

Checkpoint: user test notes + revision plan — tester, task, what broke, what changed, any shareable testimonial/dashboard artifact.

Week 11 — Demo Day Prep & Capstone Showcase

Goal: present the shipped, evaluated tool credibly; selected capstones may be shown to invited guests.

Checkpoint: final demo package — demo link, scorecard, walkthrough, one-pager, known limitations, credit-usage note, showcase/testimonial permission status.

Week 12 — Take the AI Story Back to Work

Goal: convert the capstone into professional leverage and, where appropriate, a public/semi-public proof artifact.

Checkpoint: AI portfolio page or work-ready story — problem, tool, demo, scorecard, lessons, limitations, cost/usage note, next step.

Weekly health gates

The capstone is checked at Week 0, Week 3, Week 6, and Week 9. These are review points, not extra sessions. Red/yellow students at a review point can be routed to a check-in/to-do clinic.

10. Cost, Hosting & Credit Policy (Critical)

This is the section most likely to trip up a rep — read it twice. Getting this wrong creates refund disputes and broken trust post-sale.

What the program covers

  • Approved cloud hosting for capstone applications during the cohort, as long as the student uses the approved hosting path and stays within reasonable capstone usage. Hosting continues for 2 months post-cohort, with a per-student hosting cap of roughly ₹10,000 equivalent.
  • Text-model/API credits — working assumption: ₹5,000 equivalent, allocated per student. Usable for models like OpenAI 4o mini, Claude Sonnet, Claude Opus, or equivalents.
  • Voice-model/API credits — working assumption: ₹10,000 equivalent for voice-heavy capstones, also per student. Usable for tools like Deepgram, AssemblyAI, or equivalents.

What "covered hosting" does NOT mean

  • Not unlimited production infrastructure.
  • Does not include enterprise-grade deployments, high-traffic commercial usage, custom domains, custom security reviews, or long-term post-cohort hosting beyond the confirmed 2-month window, unless separately approved.

What the program does NOT cover

  • Students' personal coding subscriptions (Cursor, Claude Code, GitHub Copilot, Replit, Lovable, premium no-code tools, premium automation plans, etc.) — these remain student-paid and are not being bulk-procured.
  • Optional paid software subscriptions, unless explicitly added to the inclusion list before launch.
  • External paid datasets, custom domains, paid design assets, personal productivity tools, or business-specific subscriptions unless separately approved.

Extra credits

Students with a justified capstone need (e.g., promising voice, customer-support, interview-analysis, or high-volume testing projects) can request additional credits — but this must be approved by the program team, never assumed by default. (Exact approval language is still being finalized — see Section 26.)

The clean one-liner for onboarding and sales: "We cover approved cloud hosting for capstone deployment (continuing for 2 months post-cohort, capped around ₹10,000 per student) and provide starter model/API credits (₹5,000 text, ₹10,000 voice, per student). We do not cover your personal coding subscriptions or optional paid tool subscriptions unless explicitly listed."

11. Voice Capstones

Voice capstones are supported but not forced on every student — this is a specialized path for workflows genuinely involving calls, interviews, customer support audio, meetings, field recordings, or spoken interactions.

Examples: sales-call analysis, customer-interview insight extraction, meeting-note assistants, coaching feedback from call recordings, support-call triage, field-audio summarization, voice-of-customer analysis.

Students may use voice/API credits equivalent to ₹10,000, subject to final usage rules, with tools like Deepgram, AssemblyAI, or equivalents (subject to change).

Voice students must be taught consent, privacy, file handling, transcription quality, speaker labels, accents, mixed-language audio, and error checking — a transcript should never be treated as automatically correct. If audio is sensitive, students submit a redacted transcript, synthetic example, or summary instead of raw audio.

AI Feedback HarnessFirst-pass review of checkpoints (completeness, scorecard, demo link, credit responsibility, evidence for claims) — a human always owns final feedback.

Students are grouped into pods of five for peer accountability (not the primary delivery mechanism). The cohort uses a traffic-light system: green (on track), yellow (solvable blocker), red (at risk of not shipping) — yellow/red students get routed to check-in/to-do sessions early.

The Ship Guarantee

Serious students will not be left stranded if they do the work and still need help finishing a usable, evaluated tool.

Qualifying conditions (all required): complete prework, attend ≥80% of live sessions, submit every weekly checkpoint on time, attend required capstone reviews, make a real attempt to build around an approved capstone scope.

If a qualifying student still doesn't have a working, evaluated AI tool by Demo Day, they get a 4-week Capstone Rescue at no extra cost — covering scope tightening, blocker triage, minimum viable deployment, evaluation cleanup, and final demo prep. It is a time-bounded rescue path, not unlimited 1:1 consulting, and does not promise a job, promotion, refund, production-grade SaaS product, unlimited API credits, unlimited hosting, or that every tool will beat ChatGPT.

Every entry requires student consent; confidential company names, private documents, customer data, and sensitive screenshots must be removed or anonymized. 16. Fit Rubric & Discovery Questions

Fit rubric

Fit levelSignal
Strong fitReal recurring task, domain knowledge, access to source material, willingness to build weekly, interest in a concrete AI artifact, and wants to show capability at work / to clients / to a founder network / senior stakeholders.
Workable fitCurious but no project idea yet (can use the approved project bank); or wants a voice project — qualify privacy, consent, audio availability, and whether it's worth voice-credit usage.
Poor fitWants a job guarantee, passive content library, deep ML theory, or a no-effort shortcut; cannot protect weekly build time; expects unlimited hosting/credits or all paid tools included.

Discovery questions

  • What is one recurring task at work that you wish AI could help you complete, review, or speed up?
  • When you've tried ChatGPT or other AI tools, what stopped you from using them seriously at work?
  • Do you already have documents, transcripts, policies, customer notes, tables, calls, examples, or workflow records that could become the source of truth for an AI tool?
  • Does your project involve text, voice, or both?
  • If voice is involved, do you have permission to use the audio or transcripts for learning and testing?
  • Who would use the tool if you built it — you, your team, a client, a customer, or someone else?
  • What would make the tool successful — saving time, reducing errors, improving quality, speeding decisions, or showing leadership?
  • Would you prefer a no-code path, a guided template path, or a more custom technical path?
  • Could you protect time each week for two live sessions and capstone work between sessions?
  • If you had a working demo and scorecard 12 weeks from now, where would you use it?

20. Objection Handling

"I cannot code."

That is exactly why the program has build lanes. Non-coders join the Operator Lane and build with AI-native and no-code tools. Technical students can go deeper, but coding is not the entry requirement.

"Are coding subscriptions included?"

No. Personal coding subscriptions and optional paid software subscriptions are not included unless explicitly listed before launch. The program covers approved capstone hosting and starter model/API credits, not every paid tool a student may prefer.

"Who pays for cloud hosting?"

Approved capstone hosting is covered during the cohort when the student uses the approved hosting path and stays within reasonable capstone usage. This does not mean unlimited production infrastructure, custom domains, high-traffic usage, or long-term post-cohort hosting.

"Are API credits included?"

Yes. The working inclusion is text-model/API credits equivalent to ₹5,000 and voice/API credits equivalent to ₹10,000 for qualifying voice-heavy capstones, per student. Exact usage, models, APIs, and redemption details will be shared closer to the cohort.

"Which models or APIs will students get?"

The program may use tools such as Deepgram, AssemblyAI, OpenAI 4o mini, Claude Sonnet, Claude Opus, or equivalent alternatives. The final stack may change based on availability, pricing, access, reliability, or vendor changes.

"Is this just another prompt course?"

No. A prompt course usually stops at better outputs. This program takes students through scoping, building, grounding, evaluating, model choice, safety, deployment, user testing, and demo preparation. Every week ends with a checkpoint on their own tool.

"Why does this cost ₹1,50,000?"

The student is getting a guided build system: live curriculum, build clinics, mentor review, templates, approved hosting, starter model/API credits, evaluation support, demo preparation, possible showcase visibility, and a bounded Capstone Rescue. The notional value is ₹5L+, and the cohort price is ₹1.5L.

"Will my tool beat ChatGPT?"

Sometimes it may. The stronger promise is that you will know where your tool works better, where it fails, and how to improve it. That is the difference between a toy demo and a credible AI tool.

"Can my capstone be presented to CEOs or CTOs?"

Strong capstones may be showcased to invited CEOs, CTOs, operators, founders, hiring managers, or relevant network guests where feasible. It creates visibility and credibility, but it is not a job-placement guarantee.

"Will I get a job after this?"

This is not a job-placement program. The outcome is capability, a shipped artifact, a demo, and a credible portfolio story. Showcase visibility may happen for strong capstones, but we do not promise jobs, interviews, or referrals.

"What if I fall behind?"

The program has weekly checkpoints, mentor review, office hours, and optional check-in rooms to catch blockers early. If you complete the prework, attend at least 80% of live sessions, submit checkpoints on time, attend required reviews, and still do not finish, you qualify for a 4-week Capstone Rescue at no extra cost.
